Firewall Friday — Answer Key 📝

🕵️‍♂️ Spot the Vulnerability – Answers

  • 1️⃣ Aarav logs into his bank account from a café’s free public Wi-Fi without using a VPN.
    👉 Vulnerability: Unsafe use of public Wi-Fi
    💡 Public Wi-Fi can be intercepted by hackers. Always use a VPN for sensitive logins.
  • 2️⃣ Sneha writes all her passwords in a notebook she keeps in her backpack.
    👉 Vulnerability: Insecure password storage
    💡 If the notebook is lost or stolen, all her accounts are at risk. Use a password manager instead.
  • 3️⃣ Raj clicks on a pop-up that says “Your computer is infected! Download this free antivirus now.”
    👉 Vulnerability: Falling for fake pop-ups / scareware
    💡 Many pop-ups are traps to install malware. Only download security software from official sources.
  • 4️⃣ Meera ignores her phone’s regular software update notifications to save data.
    👉 Vulnerability: Not updating software
    💡 Updates patch security holes. Skipping them leaves devices exposed to cyberattacks.
  • 5️⃣ Kabir uses the same password for his email, social media, and shopping accounts.
    👉 Vulnerability: Password reuse
    💡 If one account is hacked, attackers can access all other accounts. Always use unique passwords.
  • 6️⃣ Priya downloads a “cracked” version of a popular design software from an unknown website.
    👉 Vulnerability: Using pirated/untrusted software
    💡 Cracked software often contains hidden malware. Always use licensed and trusted sources.
  • 7️⃣ Dev leaves his laptop unlocked on his desk when going out for lunch.
    👉 Vulnerability: Leaving devices unattended and unlocked
    💡 Anyone could misuse, steal, or install malware on the device. Always lock your screen.

🎣✅ Phish or Legit? – Answers

  • 1️⃣ “Dear User, your account will be closed in 24 hours. Click this link to verify your details immediately.”
    👉 Phish 🎣 – Uses urgency and vague greeting (“Dear User”) to trick people into clicking malicious links.
  • 2️⃣ “Amazon: Your order #548321 has been shipped. Track it here in your Amazon app.”
    👉 Legit ✅ – Real order notifications usually come through the official app or website. (Safe only if from Amazon directly.)
  • 3️⃣ “Congratulations! You’ve been selected for a government grant. Provide your bank details to claim.”
    👉 Phish 🎣 – Government bodies never ask for bank details over email/SMS to give grants. Classic scam.
  • 4️⃣ “Your friend sent you a Google Drive link to photos from yesterday’s event.”
    👉 Legit ✅ (if confirmed) – Likely safe if expected and from a known contact. Always verify before clicking.
  • 5️⃣ “PayPal: We noticed unusual activity. Please confirm your identity by logging in at pay-pal.verify.com.”
    👉 Phish 🎣 – The fake domain “pay-pal.verify.com” is a giveaway. Real PayPal uses only paypal.com.
